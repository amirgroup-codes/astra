## Executive conclusion
Across the validated corpus, the best-supported prebiotic routes to alanine are **Strecker synthesis** and **spark-discharge/Miller-Urey chemistry**. **Hydrothermal synthesis** has moderate support, especially for alanine formation from **pyruvate + NH3** in the presence of **mixed-valence iron oxyhydroxide** under alkaline redox gradients, but hydrothermal settings also strongly overlap with alanine destruction. **Surface/UV astrochemical routes** directly support formation of **racemic alanine** in irradiated ice residues and only weak enantiomeric enrichment, while **cyanosulfidic** and **iron-sulfur** routes are retained mainly as precursor-generating or inferred/hypothesis-level routes rather than direct, quantitatively established alanine-forming pathways.

A critical unresolved issue is that the target here is **L-alanine**, whereas the validated evidence base supports mostly **racemic alanine** or alanine of unspecified handedness. Direct prebiotic synthesis of **enantiopure L-alanine** is **not established** in the mounted artifacts.

# 1. Synthesis routes

## 1.1 Strecker route
The validated network identifies the classical Strecker sequence as the strongest-supported direct route to alanine:
1. **Acetaldehyde + NH3/NH4+ + H2O** form a **carbinolamine / imine manifold**.
2. The imine/carbinolamine intermediate reacts with **HCN/CN−** to give **2-aminopropanenitrile (alanine aminonitrile)**.
3. Hydrolysis of the aminonitrile in water gives **alanine**, via **alanine amide** as an intermediate.

This route is supported in the mounted artifacts by explicit mechanistic treatment and quantitative barriers from **Yamabe et al. 2014, Beilstein Journal of Organic Chemistry**, plus hydrolytic linkage from **Miller et al. 1975, Origins of Life**. The s6 ranking places Strecker first by support score.

**Validated quantitative constraints:**
- Rate-determining deprotonation barrier: **9.6 kcal/mol**.
- Rate-determining nitrile-hydrolysis barrier: **34.7 kcal/mol**.
- Product stereochemical expectation in the absence of chiral bias: **racemic alanine**.

## 1.2 Spark-discharge / Miller-Urey route
The validated corpus identifies spark-discharge chemistry as another strongly supported route family. In the mounted reaction table, a reduced gas mixture containing **CH4, NH3, H2, and H2O** under **electric discharge** yields alanine among a broader amino-acid mixture, citing **Miller 1953, Science**. A sulfur-rich variant with **H2S** expands amino-acid diversity and still reports alanine, citing **Parker et al. 2011, PNAS**.

This route is experimentally direct in the sense that alanine is reported among products, but the mounted artifacts do **not** preserve a numeric alanine yield for the classic or sulfur-rich setups.

## 1.3 Hydrothermal route
The validated network contains two hydrothermal formation edges:
- **Pyruvate + NH3 + mixed-valence iron oxyhydroxide + redox/pH gradients -> alanine**, citing **Barge et al. 2019, PNAS**.
- **Simple carbon precursors + NH3 under hydrothermal conditions -> alanine among amino-acid products**, citing **Hennet, Holm & Engel 1992, Origins of Life and Evolution of the Biosphere**.

The strongest hydrothermal formation claim in the mounted artifacts is the Barge et al. route, where alanine formation is favored under **1:1 Fe(II):Fe(III)** iron oxyhydroxide, **alkaline** conditions, and **moderately warm temperatures**. However, the exact numerical yield was **not recovered** in the mounted artifact.

## 1.4 Surface/UV astrochemical route
The validated corpus supports direct alanine formation in irradiated astrophysical ice analogues. The mounted network states that **simple astrophysical ice mixtures + UV/VUV photons** yield **racemic alanine in organic residue** after workup/hydrolysis, citing **Bernstein et al. 2002, Nature; Muñoz Caro et al. 2002, Nature; Nuevo et al. 2008, OLEB**.

A related CPL-associated branch reports alanine after hydrolysis with **small enantiomeric excess**, citing **de Marcellus et al. 2007, Planetary and Space Science; Sharma 2022, MNRAS; 2015 OLEB alanine CPL study**. The mounted artifact summarizes the maximum enantiomeric excess in ice-alone scenarios as on the order of **~1%**.

## 1.5 Cyanosulfidic route
The validated network retains a cyanosulfidic branch in which **HCN-derived feedstocks + H2S/HS− + UV + Cu/cyanometal photoredox chemistry** generate an **amino-acid precursor branch relevant to alanine / alpha-ketoacid access**, citing **Patel et al. 2015, Nature Chemistry; Ritson et al. 2018, Chemical Communications; Sasselov et al. 2020, Science Advances**.

However, the mounted network explicitly marks this edge as **inferred**, not direct alanine closure. Thus, cyanosulfidic chemistry is supported here mainly as a **precursor-generating framework**, not as a directly demonstrated alanine synthesis route.

## 1.6 Iron-sulfur route
The validated network also retains an **iron-sulfur** route: **pyruvate (or related alpha-keto acid) + NH3 + FeS/H2S -> alanine**, citing **Wächtershäuser 1992, Progress in Biophysics and Molecular Biology; Keefe et al. 1995, PNAS; Amend & Shock 1999, OLEB**.

This route is explicitly marked **inferred** and **contested** in the mounted artifacts, with no recovered numeric alanine yield. It remains hypothesis-supported rather than directly established.

## 1.7 Downstream assembly route relevance
The validated network includes a downstream edge from **alanine monomer -> alanine oligopeptides**, citing **Kawamura et al. 2005, JACS; Kawamura et al. 2018, Astrobiology**. This is not a synthesis route to alanine itself, but it is relevant because it shows that once alanine is available, further prebiotic assembly chemistry is possible under hydrothermal-flow or mineral-influenced conditions.

## 2.2 Functional roles of the main precursors
- **Acetaldehyde** is the canonical carbonyl precursor for Strecker alanine synthesis.
- **NH3/NH4+** is the nitrogen donor across Strecker, spark-discharge feedstocks, hydrothermal amination proposals, and iron-sulfur hypotheses.
- **HCN/CN−** is central to Strecker cyanide addition and to broader cyanosulfidic precursor networks.
- **Imine/carbinolamine intermediates** are the key carbon-nitrogen intermediates preceding aminonitrile formation.
- **2-aminopropanenitrile** is the immediate direct precursor to alanine in the classical Strecker sequence.
- **Alanine amide** is the hydrolysis intermediate preserved in the DFT-supported Strecker hydrolysis sequence.
- **Pyruvate** is the key alpha-keto acid precursor in hydrothermal and iron-sulfur proposals.
- **H2S/HS−** functions as sulfur feedstock/reductant in cyanosulfidic and sulfur-rich discharge scenarios.
- **Cu/cyanometal photoredox systems** are route-specific catalytic systems in cyanosulfidic chemistry.
- **Mixed-valence iron oxyhydroxide** is the mineral redox catalyst/surface associated with hydrothermal alanine formation in the Barge et al. route.
- **FeS/FeS2/pyrite/pyrrhotite/greigite** define the mineral suite invoked in iron-sulfur world scenarios.
- **Interstellar ice mixtures** are the precursor matrices for UV/VUV-driven astrochemical alanine formation.

## 3.2 Mechanistic interpretation
### Strecker
The Strecker route is the most mechanistically explicit in the validated corpus. The mounted artifacts preserve a stepwise sequence from carbonyl activation to imine/carbinolamine formation, cyanide addition to aminonitrile, and hydrolysis through alanine amide to alanine. The preserved barriers of **9.6 kcal/mol** and **34.7 kcal/mol** are the clearest quantitative mechanistic constraints in the entire validated set.

### Spark discharge
The mechanism is less step-resolved in the mounted artifacts, but the route is experimentally direct: reduced gases under electrical discharge generate amino-acid mixtures containing alanine. The sulfur-rich variant broadens product diversity, consistent with volcanic-plume or sulfur-bearing atmospheric scenarios.

### Hydrothermal
The hydrothermal mechanism in the validated corpus centers on **pyruvate amination/reductive amination-like chemistry** under **alkaline redox gradients** and **mixed-valence iron oxyhydroxide**. The same environmental class also promotes decomposition, making kinetics and residence time central.

### Surface/UV
The astrochemical mechanism is photochemical synthesis in cryogenic ice matrices, followed by warming and hydrolysis/workup. This route directly supports alanine formation but mostly as **racemate**, with only weak CPL-associated asymmetry.

### Cyanosulfidic and iron-sulfur
These remain network-level or hypothesis-level mechanisms in the validated corpus. Cyanosulfidic chemistry is strong for precursor generation; iron-sulfur chemistry is conceptually plausible but weakly supported by direct alanine evidence in the mounted artifacts.

# 4. Mineral catalysts and surfaces

## 4.1 Mixed-valence iron oxyhydroxide
The clearest mineral-supported alanine-forming route in the validated corpus is the hydrothermal system of **Barge et al. 2019, PNAS**, where **1:1 Fe(II):Fe(III) iron oxyhydroxide** under **alkaline** and **moderately warm** vent-like conditions gives the maximum reported alanine yield within that study, though the exact yield value was not preserved in the mounted artifact.

## 4.2 FeS / FeS2 / pyrite / pyrrhotite / greigite
The validated precursor list and reaction network retain a broad iron-sulfur mineral suite: **FeS, FeS2, pyrite, pyrrhotite, greigite**. These minerals are invoked in sulfide-rich hydrothermal interfaces and iron-sulfur world scenarios, especially for conversion of **pyruvate or related alpha-keto acids** to alanine. However, the mounted artifacts characterize this evidence as **mixed**, **indirect**, and often **contested**.

## 4.3 Pyrite-pyrrhotite-magnetite in stability studies
The hydrothermal-fate edge notes that **pyrite-pyrrhotite-magnetite** appears in some alanine stability/decomposition studies. In the validated corpus, these minerals matter not only as possible catalysts but also as part of the environmental context controlling alanine persistence or destruction.

## 4.4 Cu/cyanometal photoredox systems
In the cyanosulfidic branch, **Cu/cyanometal photoredox chemistry** functions as a route-specific catalytic system for precursor generation under UV irradiation. The validated corpus does not preserve a direct alanine-forming closure under these conditions.

## 4.5 Mineral/surface coverage gaps
The validated s6 synthesis explicitly notes a **yield gap** and weaker direct support for mineral-assisted routes beyond the Barge iron oxyhydroxide case. Although the broader source list includes clay, pyrite, greigite, and magnetite angles, the mounted validated network does not preserve strong direct alanine-yield data for most of them.

# 6. Environmental context

## 6.1 Surface aqueous settings
The Strecker route fits **HCN-bearing aqueous surface waters**, primitive-ocean analogues, ponds, or parent-body aqueous environments. It requires access to **acetaldehyde, ammonia, cyanide, and water**, followed by hydrolysis.

## 6.2 Atmospheric electrical-discharge settings
Spark-discharge chemistry maps onto **lightning-driven atmospheric microenvironments**, including possibly **volcanic lightning** in sulfur-rich atmospheres. The validated corpus supports this as a plausible source of alanine-containing amino-acid mixtures.

## 6.3 Alkaline hydrothermal vent mixing zones
The Barge et al. route specifically maps onto **alkaline hydrothermal vent mixing-zone analogues** with **redox and pH gradients** and **mixed-valence iron oxyhydroxide**. This is one of the clearest mineral-environment couplings in the validated corpus.

## 6.4 Hydrothermal destruction window
The same hydrothermal class can also destroy alanine. The validated corpus notes stability only up to about **~200 °C** in some discussions, with hotter conditions and longer residence times promoting decomposition. Thus, vents may be both source and sink.

## 6.5 Subaerial UV-irradiated cyanosulfidic settings
The cyanosulfidic branch is framed in the validated corpus as **surface-compatible, non-hydrothermal**, and associated with **subaerial UV-irradiated ponds/streams**, near-neutral to alkaline pH, and Cu/cyanometal photoredox systems.

## 6.6 Interstellar/cometary ice settings
The surface/UV route is most naturally placed in **interstellar, circumstellar, or cometary ice analogues** under **cryogenic** and **low-pressure/high-vacuum** conditions. This route supports extraterrestrial delivery or pre-solar synthesis more directly than early-Earth in situ aqueous production.

# 7. Open questions and debates

## 7.1 L-alanine versus racemic alanine
The central unresolved issue is a **target mismatch**: the task target is **L-alanine**, but the validated evidence base supports mostly **racemic alanine** or alanine of unspecified chirality. No mounted route demonstrates robust prebiotic synthesis of enantiopure L-alanine.

## 7.2 Formation versus destruction in hydrothermal systems
Hydrothermal settings are internally contradictory in the validated corpus. They can generate alanine from pyruvate and ammonia under favorable redox/mineral conditions, yet also destroy alanine under hotter or longer-lived conditions. Net accumulation remains unresolved.

## 7.3 Missing yield data
Many routes lack preserved numeric yields in the mounted artifacts, especially:
- hydrothermal formation,
- iron-sulfur routes,
- surface/mineral-assisted routes,
- and some spark-discharge variants.

This limits rigorous route comparison.

## 7.4 Cyanosulfidic closure gap
The cyanosulfidic network is strong for precursor generation but weak for direct alanine closure in the validated corpus. Whether this route can be experimentally closed to alanine under plausible prebiotic conditions remains unresolved here.

## 7.5 Iron-sulfur evidential weakness
Iron-sulfur routes remain conceptually attractive but experimentally under-supported in the validated set. The gap is especially clear in the absence of preserved direct alanine yields.

## 7.6 Enantiomeric enrichment ceiling
The validated surface/UV CPL literature only supports **small ee values (~1%)**. Whether any prebiotic route in the validated corpus can exceed that level enough to matter for the emergence of biological homochirality remains open.

## 7.7 Dominant early-Earth branch
The validated synthesis asks which branch dominated on early Earth:
- **classical Strecker** from acetaldehyde/HCN/NH3,
- **pyruvate amination** in vent systems,
- or **atmospheric discharge** followed by aqueous processing.

The current validated corpus does not resolve that competition.
