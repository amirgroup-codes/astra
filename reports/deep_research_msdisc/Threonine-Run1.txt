# Prebiotic Synthesis Report: Threonine

## 1. Executive summary
This report is restricted to validated/analysed workspace content from the available s5/s6 upstream artifacts and their cited source lists. No uncited claims are introduced.

Across the retained threonine network, the strongest **direct positive experimental support** for abiotic threonine formation is the **aminomalononitrile (AMN) + electrophile(s) + hydrolysis** route, for which the gathered source notes state that **DL-threonine and allo-threonine were identified and quantitated** in Thanassi’s *Journal of Molecular Evolution* paper, although the numeric yields themselves were **not recoverable** in the available workspace because primary-source extraction failed (Thanassi 1975, *Journal of Molecular Evolution*; exact locator carried upstream as: “source access unavailable; prior paperAnalyzer attempt on PMID:2786 failed and no SI was retrieved”). A second direct positive route is **spark-discharge / Miller-Urey chemistry**, where Ring et al. reported **threonine and allothreonine** among discharge products, again without recoverable numeric yield in the validated workspace (Ring et al. 1972, *PNAS*; exact locator carried upstream as: “source access unavailable; no full text or SI retrieved in available tooling/workspace”).

Other synthesis branches remain more weakly supported. A **2-hydroxybutanal Strecker** route is chemically well motivated by general Strecker logic and by threonine/allothreonine precedent, but the gathered set did **not** retrieve a threonine-specific primary experiment directly demonstrating that exact substrate combination under prebiotic conditions. Likewise, a **glycine + acetaldehyde aldol / beta-hydroxy** route and a **sugar-derived threose branch** remain plausible but under-demonstrated in the retained evidence.

The hydrothermal literature in the retained set is more useful as a **constraint** than as a synthesis route. Threonine is reported to transform under hydrothermal conditions at **121–160 °C** (Shiota & Nakashima 2005, *Chemistry Letters*) and to undergo decarboxylation in water at **270–340 °C and 275 bar** (Li & Brill 2003, *Journal of Physical Chemistry A*). In the validated network these hydrothermal entries are therefore retained as upstream routes but interpreted primarily as **destruction/transformation**, not productive synthesis.

Across abiotic routes, the retained evidence consistently supports **mixtures involving threonine and allothreonine**, rather than selective formation of a single stereoisomer. No validated upstream artifact provided a recoverable numeric **threo/allo ratio**.

A major unresolved gap is quantitative: the final yield artifact explicitly documents that **no verified numeric threonine yield was recovered for any retained route** using the available workspace and source-access tooling. Therefore, the ranked route table below contains **no yield-backed numeric rows**; all routes are retained and explicitly marked as **yield not recoverable / not reported in available validated extraction**.

## 3. Per-route narrative

### R1. Strecker / 2-hydroxybutanal
The retained network treats this as a chemically plausible Strecker route from **2-hydroxybutanal + NH3 + HCN + H2O** through an **imine / aminonitrile** intermediate to **DL-threonine + DL-allothreonine** after hydrolysis. The route is grounded in general Strecker chemistry and in the mechanistic framing of Magrino, Pietrucci, and Saitta (2021, *Journal of Physical Chemistry Letters*), plus Strecker stereochemistry analogues retrieved in the source list. However, the validated upstream summary is explicit that **no direct threonine-specific experimental paper for this exact substrate combination was retrieved**. Accordingly, temperature, pressure, time, concentration, and numeric yield remain unrecovered, and the threo/allo outcome is only a **prediction** (“mixed threo/allo predicted; no direct measured ratio retrieved”). This route should therefore be treated as a **supported hypothesis**, not a demonstrated threonine synthesis in the retained evidence.

### R2. Cyanide / aminomalononitrile route
This is the strongest direct threonine-positive route in the retained network. The source notes for **Thanassi 1975, *Journal of Molecular Evolution*** state that reactions of **aminomalononitrile (AMN) with electrophiles**, followed by hydrolysis, yielded amino acids including **DL-threonine and allo-threonine**, and that these products were **identified and quantitated**. The validated route table preserves only qualitative conditions: **mild conditions reported qualitatively**, aqueous medium, hydrolysis step, and an HCN-rich setting as the environmental analogue. Crucially, the primary paper could not be extracted in the workspace, so the exact electrophile set, hydrolysis protocol, numeric yields, and any diastereomeric ratios remain unavailable. Even so, among retained routes this is the clearest case of **direct experimental abiotic threonine formation**.

### R3. Spark-discharge / Miller-Urey
The retained evidence identifies **Ring, Wolman, Friedmann, and Miller (1972, *PNAS*)** as the core source reporting **threonine and allothreonine** among products from electric-discharge experiments using reduced gases such as **CH4, N2, H2O, and traces of NH3**. The route is represented as gas-phase discharge with aqueous trapping/hydrolysis, relevant to lightning-driven reducing atmospheres or volcanic plume microenvironments. No numeric yield, pressure, or exact pH was recovered in the validated artifacts. Later reanalysis and review sources in the retained set (Johnson et al. 2008, Parker et al. 2011, Bada 2013) broaden the discharge context but do not, in the validated summaries, supply a recoverable threonine yield. Thus the route is directly supported for **presence**, but not quantitatively constrained here.

### R4. Aldol / beta-hydroxy route
This route links **glycine + acetaldehyde** to threonine-type beta-hydroxy amino acids. The core prebiotic source retained for this branch is **Subbaraman, Kazi, and Choughuley (1972, *Indian Journal of Biochemistry and Biophysics*)**, titled *Reactions of aldehydes with glycine as possible prebiotic events*. The validated network keeps the route because it is chemically relevant and historically proposed, but it also marks the evidence as weak: no direct threonine-specific prebiotic experiment with extracted conditions or yields was available in the workspace. Mechanistic support comes mainly from non-prebiotic threonine-aldolase literature showing that **glycine + aldehyde ⇌ beta-hydroxy-alpha-amino acid** chemistry is feasible, but those are analogues rather than direct prebiotic demonstrations. In the retained evidence, this route remains **plausible but under-demonstrated**.

### R5. Sugar-derived beta-hydroxy route
The sugar-derived branch starts from **threose or related tetroses**, then invokes amination/redox chemistry to reach threonine. The retained evidence supports the **availability of threose** under simple prebiotic conditions via the 2012 *JACS* paper on enantioselective generation of **erythrose and threose**, but does **not** provide a direct threonine-from-threose experiment. The route is therefore explicitly retained as an **inferred precursor-to-target link only**. No threo/allo selectivity, yield, or detailed conditions were recovered for threonine formation itself.

### R6. Hydrothermal transformation / degradation
This route is included because it appears in the upstream network, but it is not a productive synthesis route. **Shiota and Nakashima (2005, *Chemistry Letters*)** are summarized as showing that threonine in aqueous solution transforms under hydrothermal conditions at **121–160 °C**, producing **glycine plus other products including peptides/polymers**. The retained evidence therefore uses this route mainly as a **stability constraint**: threonine can be transformed in hot aqueous systems rather than preserved.

### R7. Hydrothermal decarboxylation / destruction
Similarly, **Li and Brill (2003, *Journal of Physical Chemistry A*)** provide direct evidence for threonine destruction in water at **270–340 °C and 275 bar**. The validated route table preserves these quantitative T/P values but no kinetic constants, because the PDF fetch failed upstream. This route strongly supports the conclusion that **deep, hot hydrothermal conditions are hostile to threonine persistence**.

## 4. Intermediates and precursors
The retained precursor/intermediate network identifies the following species as central to threonine-related prebiotic chemistry:

- **2-hydroxybutanal**: proposed aldehyde precursor for the Strecker branch leading to threonine/allothreonine.
- **NH3 and HCN / CN-**: core Strecker reagents in the hydroxybutanal route.
- **Imine / aminonitrile (threonine aminonitrile) [inferred]**: key intermediate in the Strecker branch; retained as inferred rather than directly observed in a threonine-specific prebiotic experiment.
- **Aminomalononitrile (AMN)**: central nitrile precursor in the strongest direct threonine-positive route; reacts with electrophiles and then hydrolyzes to amino acids including threonine/allothreonine.
- **Electrophilic carbonyl partners**: required in the AMN route, though the exact set was not extracted in the validated workspace.
- **Glycine**: both a precursor in the aldol route and a product of hydrothermal threonine transformation.
- **Acetaldehyde**: key carbonyl precursor in the aldol / beta-hydroxy route; its primordial availability is supported in the retained source list by the 2023 *Life* review on acetaldehyde formation/stabilization.
- **Threose / related tetroses**: upstream sugar precursors in the sugar-derived branch; supported as plausible prebiotic sugars but not directly linked experimentally to threonine in the retained evidence.
- **Complex discharge condensate / precursor pool**: intermediate product mixture in spark-discharge experiments before hydrolysis and amino-acid analysis.

Overall, the retained network supports two broad precursor regimes: **cyanide/nitrile chemistry** (Strecker, AMN) and **carbonyl/sugar chemistry** (acetaldehyde, threose), with the former better supported experimentally for threonine itself.

## 5. Mineral catalysts and surfaces
No retained route demonstrates a **threonine-specific mineral-catalyzed synthesis** with extracted conditions and yield. The mineral/surface evidence in the validated source set is therefore contextual rather than route-defining.

Relevant retained mineral/surface themes are:

- **Pyrite / FeS2 and chiral mineral surfaces**: retained as stereochemical analogues for amino-acid formation or chiral induction, including the 2024 *Nature Communications* paper on prebiotic formation of enantiomeric excess D-amino acids on natural pyrite. In the validated threonine network this supports only the possibility that mineral surfaces could influence stereochemistry; it does **not** directly demonstrate threonine synthesis.
- **Montmorillonite / clays**: retained sources include broad reviews and adsorption studies, plus dissertation-level evidence that **L-threonine was among amino acids tested for adsorption to montmorillonite** under varying pH and salts. This supports concentration/selective adsorption scenarios, not direct threonine formation.
- **Goethite / ferrihydrite / iron oxyhydroxides**: retained as relevant adsorption and vent-mineral contexts. The 2019 *PNAS* iron oxyhydroxide chimney analogue study is important for amino-acid synthesis under redox/pH gradients, but in the validated summary it reports **alanine**, not threonine.
- **Possible borate/mineral assistance in sugar stabilization**: retained only as a mechanistic possibility in the sugar-derived branch; no threonine-specific demonstration was recovered.

Thus, mineral evidence in the retained set supports three roles: **adsorption/concentration**, **possible stereochemical bias**, and **environmental plausibility of reactive interfaces**. It does not yet support a validated threonine-specific mineral-catalyzed synthesis route.

## 6. Experimental evidence and environmental context
### Direct experimental evidence for threonine formation
- **AMN/electrophile route**: direct experimental support for abiotic threonine and allothreonine formation (Thanassi 1975, *Journal of Molecular Evolution*), though numeric yields and exact conditions were not recoverable.
- **Spark-discharge route**: direct experimental support for threonine and allothreonine occurrence in discharge products (Ring et al. 1972, *PNAS*), again without recoverable numeric yield in the validated workspace.

### Indirect or inferential synthesis evidence
- **2-hydroxybutanal Strecker**: mechanistically plausible, but not directly demonstrated in the retained threonine-specific evidence.
- **Glycine + acetaldehyde aldol**: historically proposed and chemically plausible, but weakly evidenced for threonine specifically.
- **Threose-derived route**: supported only at the precursor level.

### Negative or constraining evidence
- **Hydrothermal systems**: direct evidence shows threonine transformation/destruction at elevated temperature. This makes hot hydrothermal cores poor candidates for net threonine accumulation.
- **Ice/UV photochemistry**: foundational UV-ice studies are retained, but the validated summaries do not establish a secure threonine-positive result. These environments are therefore better treated as possible **precursor** sources than as demonstrated threonine sources.
- **Meteorites / extraterrestrial delivery**: retained meteoritic sources explicitly note either **absence of threonine** in analyzed Murchison material or only **minor traces** considered likely terrestrial contamination (Engel & Nagy 1982, *Nature*; related 1982 *Nature* correspondence). This is a strong constraint on claims of abundant indigenous meteoritic threonine.

### Environmental interpretation
The retained evidence most strongly favors **surface or near-surface aqueous settings** where cyanide chemistry, aldehydes, and concentration cycles can operate:
- **cyanide-bearing surface waters / evaporative ponds** for Strecker and AMN chemistry;
- **lightning-driven reducing atmospheres / volcanic plume microenvironments** for spark-discharge production;
- **shallow ponds / wet-dry shorelines** for glycine–aldehyde chemistry;
- **surface pools with sugar formation/concentration** for the threose branch.

By contrast, **hot hydrothermal interiors** are disfavored as net threonine reservoirs because the retained evidence emphasizes degradation rather than synthesis/preservation.

## 7. Open questions and debates
1. **Where are the numeric yields?**  
   The largest unresolved gap is quantitative. The final validated yield artifact states that no verified numeric threonine yield could be recovered for any retained route using the available workspace and source-access tooling.

2. **How strong is the 2-hydroxybutanal Strecker route specifically?**  
   General Strecker chemistry is strong, but the retained evidence lacks a direct threonine-specific experiment for this exact aldehyde.

3. **What exact electrophiles and conditions were used in the AMN route?**  
   Thanassi’s paper is clearly important, but the validated workspace could not extract the exact reaction details or numeric yields.

4. **What are the actual threo/allo ratios in abiotic products?**  
   Multiple routes report both threonine and allothreonine, but no validated upstream artifact recovered a numeric diastereomer ratio.

5. **Can mineral surfaces do more than adsorb?**  
   The retained evidence supports adsorption and possible stereochemical influence, but not threonine-specific catalytic formation.

6. **Are ice photochemistry and meteorites meaningful threonine sources?**  
   In the retained evidence, these are at best indirect precursor or delivery contexts. Positive threonine claims remain weak relative to explicit absence/contamination concerns.

7. **Can cooler hydrothermal margins preserve threonine?**  
   The retained evidence strongly disfavors hot cores, but leaves open whether cooler, rapidly quenched, or mineral-buffered microenvironments could preserve small amounts.
