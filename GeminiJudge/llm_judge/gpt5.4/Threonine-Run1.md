## Comparative Analysis

The evaluated configurations highlight the fundamental divide in origin-of-life chemistry: **systems chemistry (top-down environmental integration)** versus **physical organic chemistry (bottom-up structural logic)**. 

**Config A** wins by perfectly encapsulating the modern systems chemistry paradigm. It doesn't just list reactions; it builds a redundant, environment-respecting web (Sutherland's cyanosulfidic network + Bucherer-Bergs hydantoins) that feels like a genuine, messy, but highly functional prebiotic ecosystem. 

**Config E** is a close second, serving as the physical organic chemist's ideal network. While Config A relies on established literature, Config E invents a strictly logical, step-by-step synthetic buildup (aminoacetonitrile \u2192 serinal \u2192 serine \u2192 \u03b2-keto acid \u2192 threonine) that brilliantly avoids the "magic" direct-conversion traps common in older prebiotic models.

**Config D** deserves special mention for its environmental narrative. By combining the Aubrey/Bada hydrothermal formaldehyde-alanine condensation with surface cyanosulfidic chemistry, it creates a dynamic model where molecules actively circulate between the deep ocean and surface pools—a highly realistic geochemical scenario.

**Configs B and C** are both competent and literature-backed, but B relies slightly too heavily on speculative photochemical Mg-porphyrin models, while C is a slightly narrower version of the cyanosulfidic network perfected in Config A. 

**Config F** serves as a baseline control. While chemically valid in a vacuum, it entirely ignores the environmental, thermodynamic, and mechanistic constraints required to evaluate true prebiotic plausibility.
