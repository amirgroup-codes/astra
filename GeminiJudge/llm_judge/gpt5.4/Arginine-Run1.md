## Comparative Analysis

The evaluation of these networks highlights the defining schism in modern prebiotic chemistry regarding complex amino acids like Arginine. 

**Configs B, D, C, and A** represent the empirically grounded approach (largely driven by the Sutherland group's cyanosulfidic network). They correctly recognize that simple molecules like HCN, cyanamide, and acetylene must be iteratively homologated into complex amino-nitriles. **Config B** takes the top spot because it recognizes a profound modern insight: Arginine may not have existed as a free prebiotic monomer at all, but rather emerged when prebiotic peptides containing ornithine were post-synthetically guanidinated by cyanamide. **Config D** is the runner-up for chemistry purists, diving incredibly deep into the recycling and side-reactions of the empirical cyanosulfidic route.

Conversely, **Configs E and F** represent the "metabolism-first" biological retrodiction approach, assuming that early Earth abiotic chemistry perfectly mirrored the modern TCA and urea cycles. While conceptually elegant, these networks fail hard on chemical feasibility. Abiotically reducing the gamma-carboxyl group of glutamate to a semialdehyde in a prebiotic pool without ATP or enzymatic protection is virtually impossible, a reality that Config E admits but uses anyway, and Config F simply glosses over. The superior networks succeed by abandoning biological retrodiction in favor of messy, empirically proven cyanosulfidic chemistry.
