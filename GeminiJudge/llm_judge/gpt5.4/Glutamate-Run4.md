## Comparative Analysis

The fundamental challenge revealed across these six configurations is **the tension between network completeness and chemical realism**. 

**Config A** wins decisively because it leans heavily into state-of-the-art literature (Bucherer-Bergs, modern Fe/Ni catalysis, metal-free TCA analogs) and explicitly respects the analytical boundaries of prebiotic chemistry (e.g., separating stepwise sequences rather than claiming magic one-pot yields). While it slightly truncated the origins of glyoxylate, the sheer density of accurate chemistry makes it superior.

**Configs B, C, and D** all suffer from the "complex input" fallacy. To reach glutamate, they simply insert complex C4 or C5 molecules (succinyl-thioester, α-hydroxyglutarate, 3-oxalomalate) as starting materials without explaining how the prebiotic earth synthesized them from C1/C2 feedstocks. **Config C** mitigates this somewhat through exceptional mechanistic depth regarding kinetic traps (cyanohydrins) and novel reagents (DAP), but it remains a fragmented network.

**Config E** represents the opposite extreme: it successfully builds an unbroken chain from simple CO₂ and HCN all the way to Glutamate, achieving perfect graph topology. However, to close this gap, it forces the chemistry through a highly unfavorable biological pathway (non-enzymatic succinate to α-ketoglutarate), demonstrating that perfectly connected networks are often chemically unrealistic.

**Config F** acts as a baseline failure, showing that merely summarizing one paper and using a "black box" to generate precursors does not constitute a valid synthesis network.
