## Comparative Analysis

The evaluation of these networks highlights a clear divide between **systems-level geochemical modeling** (Configs A, B, C, D) and **linear, abstracted chemical mapping** (Configs E, F). 

**The Top Tier (B and A)** succeed because they recognize that prebiotic chemistry is highly dependent on environmental micro-contexts (e.g., wet-dry cycling on clays vs. high-pressure Fe-S catalysis). **Config B** takes the top spot by integrating absolute state-of-the-art findings (Matsushita's 2024 oxyglycolate pathway) and realistic "messy" chemistry (Garakuta macromolecules), making it feel like a living representation of current origin-of-life frontiers. **Config A** is a masterclass in comprehensive baseline modeling, ensuring no major prevailing theory was left out.

**The Middle Tier (D and C)** offer fascinating, highly specialized takes on the network. **Config D** is brilliantly anchored in wet-lab systems chemistry, prioritizing realistic environmental fluxes where complex molecules degrade into simpler ones (serine to glycine). While geochemically accurate, this top-down approach makes it slightly less cohesive as a purely bottom-up *synthesis* network. **Config C** pushes maximum novelty by utilizing computational models and ice-chemistry, but trades away some experimental certainty in the process.

**The Bottom Tier (E and F)** suffer from chemical logic failures. **Config E** attempts a creative CO2-first approach but relies on speculative leaps and backward prebiotic logic (synthesizing C3 alanine just to deaminate it to C2 glycine). **Config F** serves as a baseline control, demonstrating that merely listing stoichiometric reactions without environmental, catalytic, or mechanistic context is insufficient for prebiotic network modeling.
