## Comparative Analysis

The evaluation of these networks highlights a crucial divide in origin-of-life modeling: the tension between **chemical elegance** and **geochemical/thermodynamic constraints**. 

**Config A** secures the top rank because it is the only network that explicitly recognizes and successfully mitigates the "elephant in the room" regarding threonine: it is the least stable of all proteinogenic amino acids (rapidly degrading via β-elimination). By routing the final synthesis through an ice-eutectic phase, Config A perfectly aligns chemical kinetics with environmental necessity. 

**Config E** and **Config B** represent the pinnacle of pure chemical creativity. **Config E** provides a stunningly elegant bypass to the Sutherland cyanosulfidic route by recognizing that triose sugars naturally degrade to methylglyoxal, which can be selectively reduced on FeS minerals to yield the exact precursor needed. **Config B** takes a systemic leap forward by targeting a threonine *thioester* rather than a free amino acid, seamlessly bridging the gap between prebiotic monomer synthesis and early translation/peptide formation.

**Config D** and **Config C** offer fascinating alternative topologies (Aubrey's amino-acid aldol elongation and Thanassi's AMN condensation, respectively). However, **Config D** is penalized precisely because it runs afoul of the thermodynamic constraints that Config A solved: it proposes the synthesis of thermally fragile threonine inside a boiling hydrothermal vent. **Config C** relies on an intermediate (AMN) that historically struggles with prebiotic plausibility due to runaway tar formation.

**Config F** serves as a baseline reminder of why complex network modeling is necessary; simple, linear, textbook equations fail to capture the messy, competitive reality of prebiotic geochemistry.
