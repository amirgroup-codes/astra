## Comparative Analysis
**Config C** narrowly edges out **B** and **A** for the top spot because it pushes into the true frontier of Origin-of-Life systems chemistry: the transition from pure geochemistry to early biochemistry. By utilizing very recent discoveries—specifically how Ni0 can "charge" a proto-cofactor (pyridoxal) to drive transamination, and how formamide protects vulnerable nitriles from wet-cycle degradation—Config C bridges the gap between mineral catalysis and soluble enzymatic precursors better than any other network.

**Configs A, B, and D** all share a sophisticated approach to network topology, utilizing bipartite hubs (like pyruvate and acetaldehyde) to allow hydrothermal and surface chemistries to cross-feed one another. **Config B** stands out for tackling the "dirty" reality of prebiotic chemistry: it explicitly models competing reactions (lactate over-reduction) and deals with the uncomfortable experimental fact that some minerals naturally favor the "wrong" enantiomer (D-alanine on pyrite), using RNA amplification to correct it. 

**Config F** highlights the difference between textbook organic chemistry and prebiotic systems chemistry. While F shows how to make alanine in a sterile flask, **Configs A-E** show how early Earth might have dynamically managed degradation, competing side reactions, and varying environmental conditions to allow alanine to emerge and accumulate.
