## Comparative Analysis

The evaluation of these networks highlights a fundamental challenge in prebiotic chemistry: **Glutamate is notoriously difficult to synthesize prebiotically** because its immediate keto-acid precursor ($\alpha$-ketoglutarate) is remarkably unreactive toward reductive amination compared to pyruvate or oxaloacetate. 

**Config A** wins because it explicitly acknowledges this "reactivity paradox" (Mayer & Moran 2022) and uses it to justify the network's architecture, providing massive redundancy and drawing on cutting-edge 2024 literature. **Config C** is a virtually tied runner-up; it solves the exact same problem by bypassing direct amination entirely, utilizing highly creative systems-chemistry workarounds like the Bucherer-Bergs hydantoin cycle and DAP-mediated phosphoro-Strecker chemistry. 

**Config E** distinguishes itself through extreme mechanistic fidelity—refusing to lump complex aldol condensations or transaminations into "magic" single steps, instead explicitly detailing the Lewis acid chelation and discrete $\beta$-eliminations required. **Config B** also deserves special mention for its environmental ingenuity, utilizing the reversible cyclization to pyroglutamate to protect the amino acid in destructive hydrothermal gradients. 

Conversely, **Config F** represents the pitfall of "textbook mapping"—assuming that because biology does it in an enzyme active site, it can happen in a single step on a rock. The top-ranked configs (A, C, E) succeed because they respect the kinetic and thermodynamic limitations of uncatalyzed organic chemistry.
